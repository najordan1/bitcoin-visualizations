Redundant Energy Consumption

As previously mentioned, one of the most fundamental parts of Bitcoin’s functionality is its proof-of-work model, which it uses to maintain the security of its blockchain and ensure a constant rate of production no matter how many mining computers are active. To put it in basic terms, Bitcoin is mined through the generation of blocks, which requires miners to find a hash below a target 256-bit number. Once found, a solution hash can be easily verified by other miners, but the only way to find a solution is through an extraordinarily high volume of guessing - the computational ‘work’ in question. Additionally, Bitcoin adjusts the difficulty of this target based on the amount of miners in the network, so that no matter how much computational power is used, a block will always be generated approximately every 10 minutes. While this does ensure Bitcoin is consistently produced and server-side fraud is prevented (as the low probability of finding a solution makes it impossibly difficult for bad actors to extend fraudulent chains), it also means that all Bitcoin miner are trying to solving the same problem at once, and thus a lot of the energy consumption of bitcoin is largely spent on redundant work. To demonstrate this, the following visualization has been created by taking the average daily total energy consumption of Bitcoin in 2021 (as estimated by the CBECI) and dividing it by the energy consumption of a single mining computer (5 kWh per hour, or 120 kWh when running continuously for a full day) to find the average number of active Bitcoin mining computers on a given day. Each square represents 144 computers: the red squares represent computers that solved a single block on a given day, and the blue squares represent computers that worked continuously for 24 hours without solving anything (and thus doing purely redundant work).

As we can clearly see, even assuming the best case (and most likely, since the odds of solving a block more than once in 24 hours is extremely low) scenario of every block being solved by a different computer, the amount of computers doing purely redundant work on a given day outnumber the rewarded computers by a ratio of 16,443:1. Additionally the 5 kWh figure is actually an overestimation of the energy consumption of the average mining computer, meaning that the actual number of computers mining Bitcoin, and thus the number of computers doing purely redundant work, is likely even greater than this figure shows. One could argue that, since the proof-of-work model maintains Bitcoin’s consistency and security, all of this computational power has a purpose and so, the consumption, while redundant, is not wasted. However, this fails to consider whether Bitcoin is the best use of the redundant computational power, particularly when more efficient models such as proof-of-stake exist in other cryptocurrencies. This leads us to a question: if Bitcoin requires this much redundant energy consumption to function (a number which will only increase as Bitcoin grows and more computers join the network), then is Bitcoin really worth the energy it consumes?
