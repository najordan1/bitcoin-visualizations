Bitcoin: Using Quite a Bit of Energy

The question we are trying to ask is on its face incredibly simple: How much energy, in total, has Bitcoin consumed? On its own, it would seem like that question could be answered with a simple number (52.18 billion kWh in 2019) and nothing else. However, while that answer is technically correct, it is unhelpful in explaining how much energy is consumed in a way that people can understand. How big is a kilowatt hour? Is 52.18 billion a lot? Is it unreasonably a lot? In order to properly answer the question, therefore, it becomes necessary to provide a point (or several points) of comparison. How much energy does Bitcoin consume, relative to the energy consumption of things the audience would be better able to visualize? We answer this question in the following two bar graphs, each using a different comparison: one that hopefully demonstrates the narrative we wish to show in the answer, and one that doesn't.

This first bar chart shows a comparison of the total energy consumption of Bitcoin in 2019 (using data from the Cambridge Bitcoin Electricity Consumption Index (CBECI)) with the total energy consumption of every country in 2019 (using data from the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA)) with Bitcoin highlighted in red. It should be noted that the five countries with the highest energy consumption (China, United States, India, Russia, and Japan) were excluded from this chart due to being outliers from every other country in terms of energy consumption. The implications of this chart should be clear: Bitcoin, a relatively new technology that with a niche userbase, consumes more power annually than most countries. While not the largest bar, it's visibly larger than a majority of other bars on the chart. That such a small industry is consuming so much energy on a global scale is fairly concerning, especially considering the associated environmental cost.

This second bar chart shows a comparison of the total energy consumption of Bitcoin from November 2019 to November 2021 (using data from the CBECI) with the total energy consumption of sectors of society just within the United States in the same timeframe (using data from the EIA). This graph presents an alternative view of Bitcoin's energy consumption, namely that it is a very small fraction of energy consumption in global society (given how small it is compared to the consumption in the U.S. alone). Under this chart, it seems that Bitcoin's energy consumption is almost negligible when compared to the energy consumption that already exists, and thus Bitcoin's energy consumption shouldn't be concerning (or, at least, it shouldn't be the focus of energy reduction efforts). Additionally, this chart lays bare the data manipulation performed in the first graph: if the five 'outliers', particularly China and the United States, were to be included, Bitcoin's relative height would be so small as to be indistinguishable from a majority of countries. Excluding them makes Bitcoin's place in the dataset more visually distinct, but also makes it appear to be a larger percentage of global energy consumption that it actually is.
