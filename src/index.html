Comparing Bitcoin With Countries Growing Energy Consumption

The True Cost of Bitcoin

Nathan Jordan and Ben Pelczynski

"Bitcoin is an innovative patent network and a new kind of money." This slogan is plastered in large text across the front page of Bitcoin's website. Below it, the site contains links to well-designed starter guides tout the security, privacy, and convenience of buying and selling goods with Bitcoin. Every page of the website operates in easy to follow, step-by-step instructions that help interested visitors learn about and buy Bitcoin (incidentally, the default amount that the Bitcoin website asks you to convert into Bitcoin is $300). At no point on the website is energy consumption or the environment mentioned. While the cryptocurrency market's boom of investment and speculation in the last couple years has in some ways expanded past Bitcoin, Bitcoin still holds the most value and widespread use by a wide margin, and so it is useful to focus on Bitcoin when examining the environmental costs of this new technology.

A bit of context for the unfamiliar: Bitcoin is a digital currency that, instead of having its value set by a centralized backer like most currencies, verifies transactions involving it across a decentralized network using a blockchain ledger. Importantly, Bitcoin uses a 'proof-of-work' model to validate new units on this blockchain, which requires the computers maintaining the network (the Bitcoin 'miners') to solve a complex computational problem involving the transaction, and rewards the first correct validator with a fraction of newly minted Bitcoin. This system encourages Bitcoin miners to maximise computational power on creating and validating Bitcoin transactions, and thus maximise energy consumption (other cryptocurrencies use a 'proof-of-stake' model, which is more energy efficient but still rewards maximisation of consumption).

On this site, we are using the Cambridge Bitcoin Electricity Consumption Index (CBECI), and dataset which contains and breaks down Bitcoin's daily electricity consumption as far back as 2011, to demonstrate to energy and environmental costs of Bitcoin and cryptocurrency technology. Using this dataset, we hope to answer through clear visualization the questions of how much energy Bitcoin consumes, how that consumption has been and will be trending, what the environmental implications of that consumption are, and if this technology is worth such consumption.
